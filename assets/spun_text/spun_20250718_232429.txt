**The Canoe Builder**

Dick stood on the coral ledge, his eyes fixed on the southern horizon. Behind him, the breakers of the outer sea thundered and spindrift scattered on the wind. Before him, the lagoon of Karolin stretched out like a lake, infinite, blue, and alive with the cries of fishing gulls. The forty-mile ring of coral had created a sea within a sea, a stormy expanse in heavy winds, a lake of azure in light airs. And it was all his, his people, his canoes, his empty houses where the war canoes had once rested on their rollers.

As he gazed out at the lagoon, his brow furrowed, and he turned to face the breakers on the outer beach, his eyes squinting against the glare. Beyond the sea line, invisible, lay Palm Tree, an island beautiful as a dream, yet swarming with devils.

Little Tari, the son of Le Taioi the net maker, sat nearby, watching him with curious eyes. Tari knew that the men of Karolin had been swept away by war, leaving the women and children defenseless and leaderless. But yesterday, a stranger had arrived, a man with a strange boat and a girl named Katafa, who had been blown to sea years ago. The women whispered that he was sent by the gods to be their chief and ruler.

The child asked the question that had been burning in his mind, "Taori, who are you?"

Dick's eyes clouded, as if the question had stirred the depths of his past. He couldn't answer, not really. How could he explain the strange story of his life, of being left alone with an old sailor on Palm Tree, of growing up with Katafa, of escaping from the men who had come to cut down the trees?

Instead, he gazed down at Tari, his eyes softening. "I am Taori, Tari tatu. Why do you ask?"

The child shrugged, his mind already wandering to the next excitement. "I do not know. I ask as I breathe."

Dick's gaze returned to the north, his mind haunted by the memory of the men they had escaped from. He knew they would come, eventually. They would build canoes, and they would see the lagoon's mirror-like reflection in the sky, just as he had seen it. And they would come.

As he stood there, lost in thought, a hand fell on his shoulder. Katafa stood beside him, her dark hair blown back by the wind, her eyes fixed on the northern horizon. "Look!" she whispered.

Together, they gazed out at the sea, and suddenly, Palm Tree began to materialize, a mysterious stain on the horizon, growing more vivid by the second. It was as if the island was rising from the sea, a dreamlike apparition, beautiful and yet menacing.

Their minds were untutored, unaware of the scientific explanation for the mirage, but their eyes were fixed on the place from which they had escaped, their hearts racing with the memory of the savages on the beach, the wind in their hair as they fled, and the yells of the Melanesians as they burned the schooner.

Dick's nostrils flared, his eyes darkening with hate. They had threatened him, but more importantly, they had threatened Katafa. And they still threatened her.

Some day, they would come. The vision of Palm Tree seemed to whisper it in his ear. They would build canoes, and they would see the lagoon's light in the sky, and they would come. They had no women, those men, and here were women... The thought sent a shiver down his spine.

As the vision of Palm Tree began to fade, Dick's gaze dropped to the lagoon, where a canoe was approaching from the southern beach. It was the canoe he had sent for the canoe builders, and he knew it was time to get to work. The threat from the north might be distant, but it was real, and Dick knew that he had to prepare his people for what was to come.
